Blood Banking and Transfusion Medicine

Blood Products

Product Supplied as Treatment Purpose
RBCs Packed RBC units Increase oxygen carrying capacity
Platelets Platelet packs (collected via apheresis) Control or prevent bleeding due to platelet dysfunction or thrombocytopenia
Plasma and Plasma Derivatives Fresh frozen plasma (coag factors), cryo-precipitate (fibrinogen) Treat coagulation factor deficiency, low fibrinogen (DIC)

  • Note: Whole blood transfusion rarely - instead use packed RBCs + plasma

Options When Ordering Blood

Variation Uses Details
CMV Ab Negative Blood Prevent transmission of CMV to an immunosuppressed patient In reality, CMV status is usually not tested/unknown, so in packed RBC transmission, all products are leukoreduced to eliminate WBCs
Leukoreduced Blood Leukocytes filtered out prior to storage to eliminate risks of CMV, EBV, Human T cell Leukemia/lymphoma virus CMV exists within WBCs, so removing WBCs reduces CMV risk; Also reduces risk of acute febrile nonhemolytic tranfusion reaction (host Ab vs. transfusion WBCs), less HLA alloimmuniazation (host making Abs to transfusion HLA molecules)
Washed RBCs Decreased risk of acute non-febrile, non-hemolytic urticarial transfusion reaction or other anaphylactic reaction; Reduced K+ burden for renal insufficient patients; Less IgA for IgA deficient recipients; Less transfused donor Ab (can cause TRALI)
Irradiated RBC or Platelets Reduce risk of Graft vs. Host Disease if recepient is immunocompromised Use in neonates, Intrauterine cases, leukemia/lymphomas, or if donor is close relative (increases GVHD risk)

ABO Blood Group Antigens

ABO antigens are glycoproteins and are not only expressed on RBCs.

  • L-fucosyl transferase activity is determined by H allele, and adds on H antigen
    • hh => no L-fucosyl transferase activity => no H antigen => Oh phenotype (Bombay)
  • N-acetyl galactosamine transferase => A antigen
  • D-galactosyl transferase => B antigen
  • Isohemagglutinins - Autoantibodies to ABO antigens develop in response to environmental CH2Os
    • Elicit IgM antibodies without previous exposure
    • Detectable after four months old

Rh Blood Group Antigens

Rh antigens are protein antigens and are expressed only on RBCs

  • There are 5 possible common antigenic products: C, c, D, E, e
  • D is most important
    • Rh D protein is highly immunogenic, transmembrane protein
    • Rh positive D = Rh D is present; Rh negative d = Rh D is not present
    • Rh D negative is uncommon, and very rare in Native Americans and Asians
  • RHCE gene controls expression of the CcEe transmembrane protein
    • C and c are co-dominant, and E and e are co-dominant
    • These are all expressed on the same transmembrane protein!
    • These are not typically tested for

Blood Typing

ABO blood typing is performed via observing agglutination reactions.

  • Forward typing: determining which ABO antigens are expressed on RBCs by ading anti-A and anti-B serum. agglutination => positive
  • Back typing: determining the isohemagglutinins present in patients serum by treating test RBCs with patient's serum

Rh D typing is performed via an indirect agglutination test:

  1. IgG against Rh D added. If Rh D is present, IgG will coat RBCs
  2. Anti-human globulin is added
  3. If cells were coated with IgG, they will be cross-linked by anti-human globulin and agglutination will occur

Blood products are screened for HIV, Hepatitis B and C, HTLV 1 and 2, West Nile Virus, Chagas Disease, and Syphilis

Crossmatch - the final step before transfusion, in which the donor's RBCs are mixed with recipient's plasma to observe for agglutination or hemolysis

  • If positive, must crossmatch a different unit of blood

Risks of Transfusion

Iron overload

Can occur with multiple transfusions

Alloimmunization

Alloimmunization to Rh D

  • If recipient is Rh D -, first exposure to Rh D from transfusion or pregnancy with Rh D+ fetus leads to sensitization
  • Second exposure => Rh D antibodies produced => acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (if transfused) or hemolytic disease of the newborn
    • Hemolysis in fetus causes fetal anemia => extramedullary hematopoiesis and CHF in utero => hydrops fetalis
    • Hemolytic disease of the newborn is prevented by giving Rhogam

Alloimmunization to HLA

Repeated platelet transfusions can cause platelet refractorines, sometimes due to anti-HLA antibodies in the patient's blood

  • Treatment: use only HLA-compatible platelets
  • Can cause problems for future organ transplants

Transfusion Reactions

Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions

Anti-RBC antibody reaction to transfused RBCs leading to hemolysis of transfused blood

  • Acute - within 24 hours
    • Causes: ABO or Rh D incompatibility
      • IgG against RBC antigen => Intravascular hemolysis with strong complement activation or extravascular hemolysis with weaker complement fixation
      • IgM against RBC antigen => Intravascular hemolysis with complement activation only
    • Findings:
      • Fever, chills, nausea/vomiting, SOB
      • Chest pain, flank pain, tachycardia, hypotension, oliguria
      • Burning sensation at infusion site
      • Shock, DIC, death
      • Lab findings of hemolysis + renal failure: declining Hct, decreased haptoglobin, increased LDH, hematuria, decreased urine output, increased Cr and BUN
      • DAT+
  • Delayed - after 24 hours
    • Causes: low titer of antibodies to non-ABO antigens
    • Extravascular more comman than intravascular
    • Findings:
      • DAT+
      • Anemia (potentially severe) 7-10 days after transfusion
    • Treatment: Glucocorticoids, IVIG, EPO
  • Direct and Indirect Coombs Test (DAT and IAT)
    • Direct: looking for IgG or C3 on patients RBCs directly
    • Indirect: looking for IgG in the patient's serum by treating test RBCs and anti-human IgG with the patient's serum

Acute Febrile Non-Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions

Donor plasma contains cytokines (most common) or the recipient has IgG or IgM against donor WBCs or Platelets, leading to fever.

  • Chills and fever within 1-6 hours of transfusion
  • DAT-
  • Can be prevented by using leuko-reduced RBCs
  • Most common transfusion reaction

Acute Urticarial Transfusion Reactions

Recipient has IgE against some donor protein, leading to allergic symptoms

  • Urticaria, itching, bronchospasm and anaphylaxis (more serious, can cause death)
  • DAT-
  • Treat with anti-histamines and glucocorticoids during reaction and preventatively in the future

Donor Attack On Recipient

  • Anti-RBC antibody in donor plasma => hemolysis of recipient blood
    • Not usually a problem because packed RBCs have little plasma volume, washed packed RBCs have almost no plasma volume, and plasma transfusions are checked for compatability
  • TRALI - Transfusion-related acute lung injury (most common cause of fatality from transfusion)
    • Donor plasma antibody against recipient WBC => WBC margination and activation in lung => lung injury and respiratory distress
    • Alternatively, plasma contains cytokines that activate recipient WBC
    • Findings:
      • Acute respiratory insufficiency +/- pulmonary edema, fever, chills, hypotension
      • DAT-
      • Fast onset: either during transfusion or within 1-6 hours
      • Resolves spontaneously in 1-2 days with supportive treatment
    • Risk is reduced if plasma is from males (less frequently HLA sensitized)
  • Transfusion Associated Graft vs. Host Disease
    • Viable lymphocytes in donor blood attacks HLA-incompatible cells in the recipient
    • More common in related donors
    • Signs/Symptoms include Rash, Hepatitis, Marrow failure, and abdominal pain/symptoms
    • Prevent by using irradiated blood
    • More common and more serious in immunocompromised patients
