Myeloproliferative Neoplasms

General Principles

Neoplastic proliferation of myeloid-lineage cells:

  • Granulocytes => CML
  • Erythrocytes => Polycythemia Vera
  • Megakaryocytes producing platelets => Essential Thrombocythemia
  • Megakaryocytes => Primary Myelofibrosis

Causes hypercellular bone marrow and increased peripheral blood counts.
Increases risk of hyperuricemia and gout - high RBC turnover => increased uric acid upon removal of nucleus from mature RBCs.

Affects older adults, can cause splenomegaly, can progress to marrow fibrosis (which will cause cytopenias), and can progress to acute leukemia

Polycythemia Vera

Clonal hematopoietic neoplasm of erythroid cells, strongly associated with JAK2 mutation

  • Granulocytes and platelets are also increased

Symptoms are related to increased RBC mass causing hyperviscocity of the blood:

  • Blurry vision, headache, HTN, flushed face, pruritis
  • Increased risk of venous thrombosis (Budd-Chiari Syndrome)
  • Splenomegaly, thrombosis or bleeding
  • Low EPO level (distinguishes from reactive polycythemia)

Hypercellular Marrow with prominent erythroid cells and atypical megakaryocytes

Disease Progression:

  1. Chronic Phase - indolent, 10-15 year survival
  2. Spent phase - myelofibrosis, 2-3 year median survival
  3. Acute leukemia - 1-2% of patients managed with phlebotomy progress to acute leukemia

Treatment: Phlebotomy to decrease Hct to under 45%, Hydroxyurea for high risk patients, Aspirin for low risk patients

Essential Thrombocythemia

Neoplastic proliferation of platelets, associated with JAK2 mutation (but not as strongly as PV), with persistent thrombocytosis > 600,000

  • In addition to older adults, can also affect women ~30yo

Symptoms are secondary to elevated platelets, but patients are often asymptomatic:

  • Bleeding and thrombosis
  • Mild splenomegaly
  • No increased risk of hyperuricemia/gout

Increased platelets in peripheral blood with some giant platelets. Increased hyperlobulated and large megakaryoctes in bone marrow.

  • Bone marrow cellularity may be normal or only slightly increased

Disease Progression is mild, marrow fibrosis is uncommon

Treatment: Hydroxyurea for high risk patients (thrombosis history, old), aspirin for low risk patients

Primary Myelofibrosis

Neoplastic prolifertion of megakaryocytes associated with excess Platelet-Derived Growth Factor excretion, causing marrow fibrosis and extramedullary hematopoiesis

  • Disease progresses from proliferative to fibrotic, causing different clinical and pathological findings

Pre-fibrotic Symptoms:

  • Fatigue, weakness, SOB
  • Splenomegaly, possible hepatomegaly
  • Mild anemia, moderate leukocytosis and thrombocytosis

Fibrotic Symptoms:

  • Marked hepatosplenomegaly
  • Increased blasts, Prominent tear drop RBCs and nucleated RBCs in blood

Pre-fibrotic: Hypercellular marrow with increased atypical, clustered megakaryocytes; mild reticulin fiboris
Post-fibrotic: Marrow is replaced by collagen fibrosis
