Hemolytic Anemias

General Principles of Hemolytic Anemia

Hemolysis is the accelerated rate of destruction of RBCs (less than 120 days). Can be compensated by the bone marrow if mild, causing an elevated reticulocyte count.

Hemolytic anemia is an accelerated rate of RBC destruction that the bone marrow cannot fully compensate for by increased RBC synthesis.

Clinical Presentation of Hemolytic Anemias

  • Jaundice
  • Scleral Icterus
  • Gallstones - increased billirubin
    • Many patients require cholecystectomy
  • Splenomegaly => LUQ pain, early satiety (spleen pressing on stomach)
  • Leg ulcers
  • Acute crisis (aplastic, megaloblastic)
    • ex: Parvovirus causes temporary bone marrow suppression, which (due to short lifespan of RBCs) will cause severe acute anemia
      • May see giant pronormoblast
    • Retic count will suddenly drop from increased to almost 0
  • Symptoms of tisue hypoxia, CV compensation
    • fatigue, weakness, SOB, tachycardia, palpitations

Extravascular vs. Intravascular Hemolysis

Extravascular

More common (90% of cases ). Inappropriate removal of RBCs by tissue macrophages.
Causes release of unconjugated bilirubin, iron, and protein.

Intravascular

10% of all cases. RBCs are destroyed in the general circulation, causing release of:

  • Billirubin
  • Hemoglobin => hemoglobinuria
  • Hemosiderinuria
  • Low haptoglobin
  • Higher LDH than extravascular

Mechanisms of Hemolysis - Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

Intrinsic

Reason for hemolysis is intrinsic to the RBC itself

Membrane Disorders

Ex: Hereditary Spherocytosis

  • Most common hereditary hemolytic anemia in Caucausians
  • Most commonly AD inheritance
  • Caused by mutation in membrane proteins (spectrin, ankyrin, band 3) => loss of biconcave disk shape
  • Spherocytes, increased reticulocytes, nucleated RBCs
    • Causes increased MCV
    • Nucleated RBCs are only visible in periphery when bone marrow is replacing a lot of blood
  • Diagnosis:
    • Osmotic Fragility test - measures release of Hb from damaged RBCs due to exposure to hypotonic saline solution. Normal RBCs are more tolerant to osmotic expansion than spherocytes.
  • Treatment
    • Splenectomy is very effective

Metabolic Disorders

Ex: G-6PD Deficiency

  • Most common metabolic disorder of RBCs, especially in African American males
    • Mediterranean variant also exists
    • G-6PD provides survival advantage to malaria
  • Deficiency in G-6PD => reduced detoxifying of H2O2 => failure of RBC to withstand oxidative stress
  • X-linked inheritance (more common in men)
  • Typical presentation: acute hemolytic crisis after exposure to oxidant stressors or drugs
    • Common drugs: Sulfa, antimalarials, fava bean
  • Bite cells on peripheral smear, Heinz body staining +

Hemoglobin Variants

See Sickle cell, Thalassemias

Extrinsic

Reason for hemolysis is not intrinsic to the RBC itself

Autoimmune mediated

Two Kinds:

  1. Warm - IgG mediated
  2. Cold Agglutinin - IgM mediated (binds complement)

Distinguished by Coombs Test:

  • Coombs Reagent = mix of Anti-IgG and Anti-C3 protein (for IgM)
  • Coombs Reagent is mixed with patients blood to test for agglutination with IgG and C3
Type Coombs Test Smear Clinical Features Treatment
Warm + for IgG Spherocytes, nucleated RBCs, increased reticulocytes Associated with autoimmune and lymphoproliferative disorders, destruction of RBCs primarily in spleen Steroids (first line), splenectomy, rituximab
Cold + for C3 Agglutination of RBCs Older age group, intravascular RBC destruction, Raynaud's / Acrocyanosis, Post-infectious or lymphoma Rituximab, avoid cold temps., plasmapharesis for acute emergency

Red cell fragmentation

Ex: Microangiopathic Anemias

  • High shear stress leads to damage of RBCs
  • Causes:
    • Macrovascular disease - heart valve replacement, trauma
    • Microvascular disease - DIC, thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura
  • Schistocytes noted on peripheral blood smear
